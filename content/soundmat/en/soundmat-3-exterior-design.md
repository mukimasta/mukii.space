This chapter covers SoundMat’s exterior design. Two constraints matter upfront: **structure**—electronics must fit inside, so sci-fi fantasy shapes are out; design must be buildable—and **prototype reality**—lab hand-build, limited time; nothing too wild or complex.

What follows is ideal design *within* what we can actually make—appearance first, pushing complexity where we can, then trimming during engineering.

The previous chapter ended with: *it’s there, and that alone is aesthetic*. SoundMat must read as art object—aligned with the first two user scenarios: restraint, nature, refinement.

**Overall form**

SoundMat is a pressure mat; the cavity below holds boards and electronics. The enclosure is essentially a round box. Aesthetically, an inverted truncated cone—wider on top, narrower below—works best: it sits on a smaller round table, table hidden, suggesting float.

Ideal diameter: 60–100 cm; height ~15 cm given structural limits.

**Surface material**

This is what makes it installation art rather than “weird circuit board.” The surface must conduct pressure. Two candidates: translucent silicone mat, or pile/fabric.

For the ideal product: pile fabric, light color (e.g. beige)—inviting, warm, refined.

Sides dark; ideal sides have uneven, plaster-like texture.

**Surface visual guides**

Polar guides—concentric rings and radials matching sensing layout—printed on the surface.

LED ring at the perimeter for musical progress.

**Objects to place**

River pebbles are ideal—natural weight and size variation, pleasant hand-feel, match upscale contexts. (And free—you can collect them.)

The earliest idea allowed anything—cups, etc. That works, but for long-term installation aesthetics, pebbles look far better.

**Color and character**

Restrained, natural, refined. Light top (off-white or light gray) pile; quiet invitation to touch; guide lines slightly darker, subtle; dark sides (wood tone, charcoal, black)—light-on-dark makes the top “float,” focus on the playing surface. Reads as furniture or art, not consumer electronics.
